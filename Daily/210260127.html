From last time.

Data

We have a data matrix \(X\) with \(N\) samples and \(k\) features. We add an additional column of ones to account for the bias, yielding an \(N\times (k+1)\) matrix \(\tilde{X}\).

In addition, we have \(N\) target values \(Y\) that form an \(N\times 1\) column vector.

Model

Our model is linear, we seek a relationship \[ y = m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + \cdots m_k x_k + b \]

Here the \(m_1, \ldots, m_k\) are the weights and \(b\) is the bias.

In matrix form, we seek a weight vector \(W\) (a \(k+1\)-dimensional column vector) with entries \(m_1, \ldots, m_k, b\). Given weights, we get predicted \(Y\) values \(\hat{Y}\):

\[ \hat{Y} = \tilde{X}W. \]

Loss

The loss function, which measures “how good” our model is, is the mean squared error (MSE). In terms of the \(y_{i}\) it is \((1/N)\sum _{i=1}^{N}(y_{i}-\hat{y}_{i})^{2}\).

In matrix terms it is \[ L = MSE = \frac{1}{N}\|Y-\hat{Y}\|^2 = \frac{1}{N}\|Y-\tilde{X}W\|^2 \]

Optimization

To minimize the MSE and find the “best” \(W\), we take the partial derivatives of the Loss and set them to zero.

Remember from multivariate calculus that if \(L\) is a function of \(w_1,\ldots, w_{k+1}\) then the vector \[ \nabla L = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{1}}\\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{k+1}}\end{bmatrix} \] is called the gradient of \(L\).

So we want to solve \[ \nabla L = 0. \]

Computation

Writing out the Loss using indices, we have \[ L = \sum_{i=1}^{N+1} (y_{i} - \sum_{j=1}^{k+1} \tilde{X}_{ij}w_{j})^2. \]

So

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{t}} = 2\sum_{i=1}^{N} \tilde{X}_{it}(y_{i} - \sum_{j=1}^{k+1} \tilde{X}_{ij}w_{j}) \]

This is the \(t^{th}\) entry in the column vector computed as

\[ \nabla L = \tilde{X}^{\intercal}(Y-\tilde{X}W). \]

So the equation \(\nabla L = 0\) is \[ \tilde{X}^{\intercal}(Y-\tilde{X}W)=0 \]

or

\[ \tilde{X}^{\intercal}\tilde{X}W=\tilde{X}^{\intercal}Y \]

Notice that \(\tilde{D}=\tilde{X}^{\intercal}\tilde{X}\) is a square \((k+1)\times (k+1)\) matrix. If it is invertible, we have the solution to the least squares problem.

\[ W = \tilde{D}^{-1}\tilde{X}^{\intercal}Y. \]

Geometry

Brief detour (the dot product)

Look again at the equation

\[ \tilde{X}^{\intercal}(Y-\tilde{X}W)=0 \]

Remember that the entries of \(AB\), where \(A\) and \(B\) are matrices, are the “dot products” of the rows of \(A\) and the columns of \(B\).

The entries of \(A^{\intercal}B\) are the dot products of the columns of \(A\) with those of \(B\).

Therefore this equation says that we should find \(W\) so that the columns of \(\tilde{X}\) have dot product zero with the columns of \(Y-\tilde{X}W\).

This means the line joining \(Y\) and the chosen \(XW\) is perpendicular to the space spanned by the columns of \(X\).

Centered coordinates

Remember that the geometric significance of the equation \[ \tilde{X}^{\intercal}(Y-\tilde{X}W)=0 \] is that, for the least squares solution \(W\), the columns of \(\tilde{X}\) are perpendicular to the vector joining \(Y\) and \(\tilde{X}W\).

On of the columns of \(\tilde{X}\) is the “all ones” column \(E\).

So \(E\cdot(Y-\tilde{X}W)=0\) at the solution point \(W\).

Notice that \(E\cdot Y\) is the sum of the \(y_{i}\), and \(E\cdot\tilde{X}W\) is the sum of the predicted \(y\) values \(\hat{y}_{i}\).

So we see that the mean of the predicted values \(\hat{y}_{i}\) equals the mean of the target values \(y_{i}\) at the solution point.

In fact what we get is that \[ \overline{y} = m_1\overline{x}_{1} + \cdots + m_{k}\overline{x}_{k} + b \] where \(\overline{y}\) is the mean of the target values and \(\overline{x}_{i}\) is the mean of the \(i^{th}\) feature.

This tells us that we can simplify our problem by changing coordinates at the beginning so that the features and the target all have mean zero!

If we do this, then we know that \(b=0\) and we no longer need to add the column of ones to our matrix, we can work with \(X\) directly.
